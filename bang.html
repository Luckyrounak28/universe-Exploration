The Big Bang

The origin of the universe, a massive explosion that started it all.

What is the Big Bang?

The Big Bang is the leading explanation about how the universe began. At its simplest, it talks about the universe as we know it starting with a small singularity, then inflating over the next 13.8 billion years to the cosmos that we know today.

The Birth of the Universe

In the first second after the universe began, the surrounding temperature was about 10 billion degrees Fahrenheit (5.5 billion degrees Celsius). The cosmos contained a vast array of fundamental particles such as neutrons, electrons, and protons. These decayed or combined as the universe got cooler.

This early soup would have been impossible to look at because light could not carry inside of it. Over time, however, the free electrons met up with nuclei and created neutral atoms. This allowed light to shine through about 380,000 years after the Big Bang.

Formation of Galaxies and Stars

Roughly 1 billion years after the Big Bang, the first galaxies began to form. These galaxies were composed of gas and dust, which eventually coalesced to form stars. The formation of stars and galaxies marked the beginning of the universe as we know it today.

Expansion of the Universe

The universe has been expanding ever since the Big Bang. This expansion is not like an explosion of matter into empty space, but rather the expansion of space itself. This means that galaxies are moving away from each other as the space between them expands.

Current Understanding and Future Exploration

Today, scientists continue to study the Big Bang and the early universe using advanced telescopes and other instruments. The more we learn about the Big Bang, the more we understand about the origins of the universe and our place within it.
